OpenAI just submitted a frontier model to the US government for approval before letting the public use it.

Tomorrow it launches GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra, and Luna publicly. Sol is priced at $5/$30 per million tokens, Terra matches GPT-5.5's performance at $2.50/$15, Luna runs budget deployments at $1/$6.

None of that shipped on schedule. Under the AI cybersecurity order Trump signed in early June, OpenAI first released GPT-5.6 only to a small group of government-approved partners. The Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation ran additional testing, and OpenAI sent technical experts to DC to answer questions in person. Only after that cleared did the public release get greenlit — weeks ahead of the order's 30-day review window, not after it.

If you deploy AI in a regulated industry, this is the case study: the question is no longer just "does this model perform." It's "who approved it, and what did they check?"

Ask your AI vendor the same question you'd ask about a subprocessor: what's your clearance process, and can you show it to an auditor? The clearance process just became part of the product.
