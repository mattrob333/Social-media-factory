Delaware just proposed the AIC — a legal entity whose day-to-day is managed by an AI agent, not a person. It can sue, be sued, hold property, and incur obligations. It has a single member responsible for capital. It keeps a log.

This is the first serious attempt to make AI legible to law. Not a policy paper — a real entity form, with a 30-month sandbox, oversight from the Secretary of State, Attorney General, and Chancery Court, and a liability shield that only holds if you follow the rules.

I've been writing that every agent you deploy is a non-human identity with permissions and no org chart entry. The AIC is a cleaner answer than I expected: give the agent a legal identity, and now you have a target for responsibility, damages, and audit.

The hard part isn't the entity form. It's that most orgs deploying agents today can't answer "who owns this agent?" much less "what entity is this agent acting for?" An AIC only works if you already know who does what — and most agent deployments are running on vibes and a Playwright script.
