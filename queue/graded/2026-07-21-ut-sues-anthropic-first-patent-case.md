Same week a judge approved Anthropic's $1.5B copyright settlement, UT hits them with the first patent infringement case — over neural network technology invented by university professors.

The legal profession is moving from "you trained on my data" to "your model IS my invention." That's a wider aperture.

Copyright covers the inputs. Patents cover the architecture. If courts start treating neural network patents as enforceable against deployed models, every frontier lab with a transformer-derived architecture suddenly has an IP liability they didn't price.

The AI industry is learning what pharma learned decades ago: the cost of freedom-to-operate isn't zero, and it compounds with every generation of model. Labs that aren't building a patent-IP line item into their cap table are understating their risk by an order of magnitude.

Not a doomer take — just a governance one. The lawsuits don't need to win to change behavior. They just need to survive summary judgment.
