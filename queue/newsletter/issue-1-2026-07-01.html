The Take of the Week

Model access just became a governance problem, not a capability problem

OpenAI shipped GPT-5.6 Sol and Terra this week — Sol a capability step-up at the same price as 5.5, Terra matching 5.5's performance at roughly half the cost. Neither shipped to everyone. Both launched as a limited preview after a government-requested review.

Read past the spec sheet and the real story isn't the benchmark delta. It's that frontier model access is now policy-gated, not just capability-gated. For two years the industry narrative has been "the best model wins." That's no longer sufficient. Now the question an enterprise buyer has to answer before they even evaluate a model is: who approved us to have this, and can we prove it if asked.

That's not a hypothetical for regulated industries. Insurance, healthcare, aerospace, and finance already run on approval chains for everything else that touches customer data or safety-critical systems. AI access is catching up to that reality whether vendors are ready or not. If your AI rollout plan doesn't already have an answer to "who approved this, and what's the audit trail," you're not behind on capability — you're behind on the thing that will actually get asked in a vendor review or an audit.

Checklists beat genius. The cockpit settled that decades ago. AI ops is relearning it the hard way, one policy-gated model release at a time.

Free Skill of the Day

Turn any GitHub repo into a one-paragraph build brief

Next time you find a trending repo and think "could I ship a wedge off this," don't just star it. Paste this into your AI tool with the repo link:

"Read this repo's README and top issues. In one paragraph: (1) what gap it fills, (2) who's actually paying for a worse version of this today, (3) the smallest wedge I could ship in a week that a business would pay for, (4) what I'd have to add that this repo doesn't have. Be concrete — no 'game-changer' language, name the actual missing piece."

That fourth question is the one that matters. The gap between "cool open-source project" and "sellable wedge" is almost always something boring: governance, support, a UI a non-technical buyer can use, or compliance. Find that gap and you have a business, not a bookmark.

The Governance Corner

Who approved this?

OpenAI's Sol/Terra preview gate (see Take of the Week) is this week's clearest example: a frontier lab just demonstrated, in public, that "we shipped the best model" isn't the whole story anymore — "who signed off on giving it to you" is now part of the release itself.

Every AI agent your company deploys is the same question at smaller scale. It has credentials. It has permissions. It can act on systems. In most orgs it has no manager, no review, and no line on the org chart. Segregation of duties didn't stop mattering because the employee is a model.

The Open-Source Swap

Trending this week — worth an operator's attention

Four things earned real attention this week. Not ten — most "top 10" lists exist to hit a round number.

  • diegosouzapw/OmniRoute +459 stars today
    Free, self-hostable AI gateway routing across 231+ model providers (50+ free), with auto-fallback and 15-95% token compression. Model routing — the thing companies were paying for a year ago — just became a config file. Worth evaluating as a cost/fallback layer under existing agent deployments, not a replacement for them.
  • obra/superpowers +884 stars today
    An agentic skills framework + dev methodology packaged as a forkable repo — with Claude itself listed as a contributor. Same shape as any serious internal skills library: procedural memory the agent can actually reuse, not just a prompt.
  • browser-use/video-use +722 stars today
    The same pattern that made browser automation agentic — the agent drives the app's real interface instead of a bespoke integration — now applied to video editing. Watch this pattern. It's spreading to every category of software.
  • google/agents-cli +433 stars today
    Google's official CLI for building, evaluating, and deploying agents on GCP. The cloud vendors are fighting over who owns the "agent deployment surface" the same way they fought over Kubernetes distros a decade ago.
